Orbital Clutter, Debris, and the Kessler Threshold

This project frames near-Earth orbit as an active systems problem: not simply a crowded sky, but a dynamic mesh of operating satellites, legacy hardware, spent rocket bodies, and fragments capable of triggering nonlinear collision chains.

Abstract

Despite most depictions of space showing an empty, nearly infinite void, the space near Earth is quickly becoming an unmanaged traffic jam of megaconstellations with tens of thousands of satellites and space debris from old missions. If left unmanaged and uncleaned, the probability of orbital collisions will only increase. These collisions create massive clouds of untrackable debris that can go on to impact other objects, creating a cascading effect. This cascade is known as Kessler Syndrome, and poses a significant threat to both current and future space development. At certain altitudes, objects can remain in orbit for decades or even centuries, potentially blocking or hindering future launches for generations. The loss of satellites would significantly impact day to day life and critical communications infrastructure including maritime / aviation communications, rural internet access, atmospheric monitoring, wildfire tracking, national security, and in worst-case-scenarios even GPS systems. There have been proposed solutions to this problem ranging from proactive international treaties limiting the post-mission lifespan of satellites to physical removal of debris and dead satellites. Efforts to curtail the risk of cascading orbital debris remain in their infancy, and with the exponential growth of satellite deployments every year, the future of our skies remain uncertain.
