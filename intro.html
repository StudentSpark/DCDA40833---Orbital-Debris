Orbit Is Becoming a Traffic System Without Lanes

This research project examines orbital debris as a compounding systems hazard. Earth orbit now contains active spacecraft, retired satellites, rocket bodies, and fragmentation remnants that share finite trajectories at high velocity. As crowding rises, conjunction management becomes more difficult, collision probability climbs, and mission resilience declines.

The analysis centers on the Kessler Syndrome: a feedback process in which collisions generate debris that increases the chance of further collisions. We investigate where crowding is most severe, how density varies by orbital regime, and what these patterns imply for long-term access to near-Earth space.

Abstract Scope

This abstract introduces the scope, purpose, and direction of the project: to evaluate orbital debris crowding as an infrastructure and sustainability problem. The study combines publicly available satellite catalog data with orbital mechanics interpretation to characterize not only object count, but also clustering, persistence, and interaction risk. The goal is to connect measurable orbital conditions to practical decisions about mitigation, active debris removal, and policy responses needed to reduce long-term instability in key orbital bands.

In the pages that follow, the literature review situates the research in existing scholarship, the methodology explains collection and analysis choices, the findings section reports observed patterns, and the conclusion evaluates how well the project answers its research question.

Critical premise: if collision generation outpaces cleanup and deorbiting, the system can transition from congested to self-amplifying.
