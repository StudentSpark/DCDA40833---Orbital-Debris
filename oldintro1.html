Near-Earth Orbit Is Entering a Saturation Era

This research introduces orbital debris as an escalating systems challenge shaped by launch growth, legacy hardware, and fragmentation. Orbit is no longer sparse: active satellites, inactive vehicles, and persistent fragments now share constrained altitude bands, raising collision exposure across civil, commercial, and scientific operations.

The central concern is the Kessler Syndrome, where collisions generate debris that drives additional collisions. We examine crowding, persistence, and distribution to identify where cascading risk becomes most likely and what that means for long-term orbital sustainability.

Abstract Scope

The project combines satellite catalog records, trajectory context, and systems interpretation to describe how debris fields evolve with increasing orbital activity. Beyond object counts, the study emphasizes where objects cluster, how long they persist, and how crowding modifies operational risk and mission planning complexity.

Core hypothesis: without stronger mitigation and disposal behavior, congested regimes can shift toward self-reinforcing collision generation.
