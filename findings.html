Visualizations and Interpretation

From the mid 20th century to the early 21st century, there has been an exponential growth of active satellites in orbit, especially in the last few years. At the beginning of 1960, there were two active satellites in orbit. In 1980 there were 304. In 2000, there were 2,283. As of April of 2026, there are currently 15,439 satellites in orbit. Of this recent massive increase in numbers, the vast majority (a little over 10,000) are part of Starlink’s mega constellation. As other corporations and countries plan to bring their own megaconstellations online, this number is likely to skyrocket even further. Blue Origin plans to place approximately 50,000 satellites in LEO (CITE), and Starlink announced further plans to place a million “AI Datacenter” satellites in a higher orbit. (CITE).

In the discussion with Dr. Matney, he overcrowding is a risk, the but a greater risk comes from uncontrollable dead satellites. While these satellites may have advanced and sometimes automated collision avoidance algorithms, the sheer number of them raise the risk of technical failures or collision with untracked debris. (CITE?) Recently, several Starlink satellites suffered as-of-yet unexplained explosions, including Starlink 34343 on March 29, 2026. (CITE). While small in number, these incidents demonstrate the inevitable risk of malfunctions present at industrial scale.

Using data gathered from CelesTrak around early March and CesiumJS, this map has been created to visualize the current orbital environment of satellites. By default, it tracks around 1000 satellites over the course of 90 minutes. While only one fifteenth of the total number of active satellites currently in orbit, it demonstrates the various layers of orbits as well as the vast number of active satellites.

When discussing various orbital altitudes at risk for debris, Dr. Matney breaks down the distinctions well:

The first key to understand is the lifetime of an object due to atmospheric drag is a function of orbit altitude. At ISS altitude (400 km), the lifetime of a satellite is months to years. Up at HST altitudes (600 km), it is decades. Around 800 km, it is more like centuries. Above 1000 km, natural lifetimes are essentially infinite by human standards (thousands of years or more). People tend to launch satellites where drag is small, so ~800 km and above. So there are lots of objects, both active and dead, at those high altitudes. There have been lots of explosions there, plus collisions, so it is the most congested region. Energetic breakups - collisions and explosions - mostly affect the immediate altitude, but spread debris all around. By the way, I am keeping this simple for convenience. I am talking about circular orbits. There are highly elliptical orbits that have many complicating factors, but right now near-circular orbits dominate the risk. (Matney).

As it stands, the missions at the greatest risk of space debris are manned missions, especially space stations. Both the International Space Station and the Tiengong Space Station orbit at low altitudes. At the low altitudes of 400 km, the two space stations frequently maneuver to avoid large debris, and their exteriors are pockmarked with impacts from small debris. Currently, the ISS has to maneuver around large debris once a year, but if the amount of debris grows, it may become impractical to maintain manned missions in orbit. (CITE)

There have been many proposed and implemented solutions to preventing, minimizing, and removing debris from orbit, but the problem is far from fixed. Dr. Matney notes that even if launches were to stop today, the debris environment would continue to grow,

The problem that most people do not understand is the time scale over which it occurs. It evolves in slow motion by human scale, but it is inevitable, given enough time, and lacking any changes to how we do things. One way we show that is to take the current population and project it forward, assuming no new launches. The collision environment grows inexorably. But, of course, we are not stopping launches. We continue to launch satellites into space faster than they are removed by natural drag. So, it is a dynamic and complicated projection challenge.

One major recent change is the implementation of the 5-year rule, an international recommendation to limit the life of a satellite to five years after its mission is complete. As this is a recommendation and not a binding international treaty, space agencies are not forced to follow this rule. (CITE).

Several companies and organizations such as the Luxembourg based ClearSpace and Japan based Astroscale Holdings have designed and proposed various solutions to removing orbital debris, ranging from satellites with claws, magnests, or tentacles to ground based lasers to push debris around. However, none of these proposals have been tested in orbit yet, so the feasibility of these systems is currently unknown. With the advent of megaconstellations, there has been increased interest in the field, but most projects remain in their infancy. (CITE)
